Introduction

Consequent upon the unbundling of the erstwhile Madhya Pradesh State Electricity Board (MPSEB), the three Power Distribution Companies (Discoms), viz. Madhya Pradesh Madhya Kshetra Vidyut Vitaran Company Limited (MPMKVVCL), Madhya Pradesh Poorva Kshetra Vidyut Vitaran Company Limited (MPPoKVVCL) and Madhya Pradesh Paschim Kshetra Vidyut Vitaran Company Limited (MPPaKVVCL) were incorporated (May 2002) and entrusted with the activity of power distribution in the State of Madhya Pradesh.

To achieve the objective of providing reliable and quality power for agriculture, households and industries at competitive rates, Discoms need to be financially sustainable and commercially viable.

Organisational set up

All three Discoms are under the administrative control of the Department of Energy, Government of Madhya Pradesh (GoMP). Each Discom is headed by a Managing Director (MD) and day-to-day activities of the field units of these Discoms are monitored by Chief General Managers (CGM) at Corporate Office.

Audit objective

The compliance audit was conducted with an objective to assess whether:

  • The reporting of Billing and Collection (B&C) efficiency was done as per the prescribed Rules and accurately; and
  • Billing and Collection of revenue was being done as per provisions of Electricity Supply Code and applicable Tariff Orders from time-to-time.

    • Audit Criteria

    The criteria adopted for audit were derived from the following sources:

  • The Electricity Act, 2003 and Madhya Pradesh Electricity Supply Code 2013 (Supply Code);
  • Retail Supply Tariff Orders (Tariff Orders) issued by Madhya Pradesh Electricity Regulatory Commission (MPERC) from time to time;
  • Agenda and Minutes of Meetings of Board of Directors (Board) of the Discoms;
  • Circulars/ orders issued by CGM (Commercial) at Corporate Office; and
  • Orders/ Guidelines/ Circulars/ Instructions issued by the Department, GoMP and MPERC.

    • Failure in achieving the target of billing efficiency fixed by MPERC

    As stated in Para 2.2.6 above, all the Discoms registered the distribution losses over and above the targets fixed by MPERC. The excess losses ranged between 6.63 to 20.23 per cent in MPMKVVCL, 4.61 to 14.56 per cent in MPPoKVVCL and 1.09 to 1.87 per cent in MPPaKVVCL during 2016-17 to 2018-19

    Out of the 15 selected field units, 12[1] field offices failed to reduce the distribution losses up to the target levels fixed by MPERC(Annexure 3).

    Thus, failure of the Discoms in complying with the losses’ trajectory level fixed by MPERC resulted in loss of ` 5,308.27 crore (MPMKVVCL- ` 2,741.99 crore, MPPoKVVCL- ` 1983.34 crore and MPPaKVVCL- ` 582.94 crore) during 2016-17 to 2018-19 in respect of 12 selected field units(Annexure 3).Audit in the selected 15 field units observed that billing efficiency was low mainly due to the following reasons:

  • Shortfall in meterisation:Despite directives issued (2016) by MPERC for 100 per cent meterisation of Distribution Transformers (DTRs)[1], 78.13 per cent[2] were not metered and 57.31 per cent[3] of the metered DTRs were lying defective (Annexure 4) as of 31st March 2019;
  • AMR based meter reading: Automatic Meter Reading (AMR) based meter reading is an important tool for increasing billing efficiency. However, 36.11 per cent LT high value consumers[4](Annexure 5) were not being billed (March 2019) using AMR either due to absence of AMR enabled meters or installation of meters without modem[5]; and
  • Capacitor Banks (CBs): These are the equipments installed at the sub-stations (SSs) to save energy and minimise distribution loss. CBs were not installed in 384 SSs out of total 1,565 SSs in 15 selected field units (Annexure 6). Had the CBs been installed, the Discoms could have saved 2181.89 MUs[6] in a year only. In addition to these deficiencies, the field units of the Discoms also failed to take up the other elements required for increasing billing efficiency which are discussed in Para 2.2.11 and 2.2.12.
